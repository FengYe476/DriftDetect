# 1. Introduction

World models such as DreamerV3 and DreamerV4 enable agents to improve policies by imagining future trajectories inside a learned latent dynamics model [CITATION]. A critical assumption behind this procedure is that imagined trajectories remain faithful to the trajectories that would be observed in the environment over the planning horizon. This assumption is violated by latent drift: the accumulation of open-loop prediction error during imagination.

Latent drift is especially consequential in recurrent state-space models (RSSMs), where the model repeatedly feeds its own predicted latent state back into the transition. Small one-step errors can compound into large trajectory deviations, even when reconstruction quality and short-horizon prediction appear adequate. Prior work has primarily characterized this failure mode through aggregate prediction error, rollout likelihood, or downstream control performance [CITATION]. These measures reveal that drift occurs, but they do not identify which temporal components of the latent trajectory are unstable. In particular, they do not distinguish slow trend error from fast oscillatory error, even though these components may have different causes and require different interventions.

DriftDetect introduces a frequency-domain diagnostic protocol for measuring imagination drift in world models. The method compares posterior latent trajectories against open-loop imagined trajectories over a fixed horizon, extracts the deterministic latent state, projects it onto principal components, and decomposes per-step latent error into five temporal bands: DC/trend, very-low, low, mid, and high. Band-specific error is measured after filtering the PCA-projected deterministic trajectories, producing a frequency-resolved account of where imagination departs from posterior inference. This protocol shows that drift is not temporally uniform. Instead, error is strongly frequency-asymmetric and concentrated in slow components.

The empirical study establishes five main findings. Finding A shows that drift is trend-dominated in DreamerV3. The DC/trend band carries 46.7% of latent mean-squared error on Cheetah Run and 97.0% on Cartpole Swingup, while high-frequency error accounts for only 4.2% and 0.3%, respectively. This pattern is robust across filter choices and episode phases, indicating that it is intrinsic to DreamerV3 RSSM imagination rather than a filtering artifact. Finding B shows that the pattern generalizes cross-architecturally: DreamerV4's transformer-tokenizer world model exhibits even stronger trend dominance, with 63.9% of latent error in the DC/trend band. Finding C shows that trend dominance is present from the first archived training checkpoint. At step 5000, the DC/trend share is 79%, and it remains the dominant band through 500k steps, ruling out late-training saturation as the source of the effect. Finding E rules out a natural decoder-based explanation. A trained decoder amplifies off-distribution latent drift by approximately 48% relative to a random decoder, but it does not preferentially amplify the DC/trend direction; indeed, the effective Lipschitz constant is smallest along DC/trend in both V3 and V4. Finally, the Mismatch Finding shows that posterior slow features and imagination drift directions are not the same subspace. Posterior/drift overlap is low in DreamerV3, ranging from 0.06 to 0.28, moderate in DreamerV4, ranging from 0.41 to 0.64, and complexity-dependent in toy models. Linear toy experiments support a capacity-gap mechanism: posterior covariance and open-loop drift covariance are governed by different operators, and their alignment decreases as model-environment mismatch increases.

These diagnostics motivate Slow-Mode Anchored Dynamics (SMAD), a zero-parameter intervention that damps the RSSM per-step update along an empirical drift subspace. Fixed-basis SMAD is safe: task performance is preserved, with no representation collapse. However, its training-time effect is limited, reducing DC/trend error by only 4.8%, far below the 20% threshold suggested by post-hoc damping. A staleness analysis explains this gap. After SMAD training, the drift subspace is nearly orthogonal to the original basis, with overlap 0.005-0.022 across ranks 3 to 10; re-estimating the basis recovers 22.6% DC/trend reduction, while the old basis achieves only 0.9%. This motivates Adaptive-SMAD, which periodically re-estimates the drift basis during training. This paper makes four contributions:

- A frequency-domain diagnostic framework for characterizing imagination drift in world models.
- Five empirical findings, A, B, C, E, and Mismatch, that characterize the structure and origin of drift across tasks, architectures, training time, decoder behavior, and subspace geometry.
- A theoretical result, Theorem 1, on slow subspace identifiability, together with numerical evidence for a posterior-drift mismatch conjecture under capacity-bounded linear dynamics.
- SMAD and Adaptive-SMAD, diagnostic-driven interventions that target the empirically identified drift subspace.
